## 1. Course description 🟢

Product Development Studio is a **hands-on, team-based course** in which students conceive, design, and prototype a real or realistic software product over two months, applying lean product methodology, user research, rapid iteration, and cross-functional collaboration. The course bridges theory (from prior PE electives) and capstone delivery, teaching the **judgment and communication** that separates a builder from a product leader. Every student participates in live design reviews with faculty mentors and, where possible, with industry partners. The studio models a **post-graduation product squad**: team ownership, sprint planning, customer empathy, and ship-or-learn velocity.

## 4. Course objectives 🟢

- Master lean customer discovery and user-centered problem framing.
- Execute a full product iteration cycle (plan → build → test → ship → learn) under real constraints.
- Collaborate cross-functionally (backend, frontend, design, ops) and resolve technical/team friction.
- Communicate vision, progress, and outcomes to peers, mentors, and (where possible) end users.
- Reflect on team dynamics, personal strengths, and path to a product role vs a specialist role.

## 6. Pedagogy 🟢

- **Studio-based (project-centred)** — students work in squads (4–5 per team) on one shared product brief or choose from 2–3 curated briefs aligned to recruiter needs.
- **Agile sprint cadence** — one-week sprints; daily 10-min standups (async Slack optional for rural/remote); sprint reviews and retrospectives on Fridays.
- **Mentored delivery** — faculty and industry mentors rotate through team standups and design reviews; feedback in-the-moment, not post-hoc.
- **Peer teaching** — advanced students mentor newer team members in architecture, testing discipline, or go-to-market strategy.
- **Real feedback loops** — user testing (beta tester interviews, NPS survey, or demo to a sample cohort) built into sprint cycle.

## 8. 🔵 Differentiated instruction (mapped to the two levels)

**Reaching the floor (awareness level) — for students at risk of not clearing it:**
- **Paired programming** — advanced team members partner with peers on high-value features first two sprints.
- **Role rotation** — every student leads at least one sprint as de-facto product lead; scaffolded with a mentorship checklist.
- **Worked example sprint** — week 1: faculty demonstrates a full sprint plan for a toy product (e.g. 2-day build); then students plan week 2.
- **Curated readings + recordings** — asynchronous, just-in-time Slack threads on CI/CD setup, testing patterns, API design (low-touch entry points).
- **Peer code review** — advance reviews by advanced students; focus on "does it meet definition of done" rather than perfection.

**Reaching the ceiling (advanced level) — for students aiming above 8 CGPA:**
- **Lead a pod** — advanced students own backlog refinement, velocity calibration, and stakeholder communication (user interviews, mentor alignment).
- **Mentor interns** — if interns are co-located, advanced students mentor on architecture decisions and teach testing discipline.
- **Extended briefs** — optional: add a go-to-market strategy, competitive analysis, or roadmap planning sprint.
- **Industry project option** — (if available) choose a brief from a live recruiter or partner company; real user feedback; potential internship pathway.
- **Reflection paper** — final reflective artifact: connect product lessons to career choice (PM vs. engineer vs. entrepreneur); propose a post-graduation project plan.

## 13. Academic integrity policy 🟢

**Code of conduct:** Plagiarism, unauthorized use of AI code-generation tools (e.g., uncredited Copilot suggestions), or dishonest peer review will result in zero marks for that assignment. If the infraction is systemic, the student may be removed from the studio and assigned a standalone project (penalty: letter grade down).

**Acceptable use of AI:** GitHub Copilot is a permitted tool for routine completions (boilerplate, tests). The student must:
1. Cite Copilot suggestions in commit messages (`Co-authored-by: GitHub Copilot`).
2. Understand and manually test every Copilot-generated code segment.
3. Not submit Copilot suggestions for novel algorithms without human review.

**Peer feedback ethics:** Peer code reviews and sprint retrospectives must be honest and respectful. Feedback must focus on the work, not the person. Conflicts are escalated to faculty, not suppressed.

### 14.1 🔵 The calibration that enforces the CGPA rule

> **Design constraint.** Set the awareness/advanced split so that a student who masters **only** the awareness portion lands **below the 8-CGPA-equivalent threshold** for this course, and reaching above it **requires** advanced marks.

| Check | Entry |
|---|---|
| Marks-to-CGPA conversion used | REVA 10-point scale (80+ = 8 CGPA; 70–79 = 7 CGPA) |
| Max % achievable from awareness marks alone | ≈54% (awareness floor: ~54 marks) |
| Grade band that % falls into | 7–7.5 CGPA band — **below 8-CGPA threshold** ✅ |
| Advanced marks needed to cross 8 CGPA | ≈26+ marks from the 46-mark advanced pool (56%+ of advanced) |
| ✅ Calibration confirmed (awareness-only < 8 CGPA) | **Yes** |

> **Verification:** A student who scores full marks on all awareness components (54 marks = 54%) lands in the 7 CGPA band. Crossing the 8-CGPA line (80%) requires picking up ≈26 of 46 advanced marks — achievable for strong contributors but not automatic. This ensures the studio reward scheme aligns with institutional CGPA signals.

## 19. Remediation & enrichment pathways

**For students at risk of not reaching the floor (awareness):**
1. **Week 0 cohort**: Assigned to a "Studio Foundations" async cohort; recorded walkthroughs of discovery + agile basics (3 videos, 20 min each).
2. **Paired sprint 1**: Paired with an advanced peer for first two sprints; explicit teaching of story writing and velocity estimation.
3. **1-1 mentor check-in** (sprint 3): Faculty mentor session (30 min) to assess readiness; if not on track, offered a "contributor role" (no product ownership) in sprint 4+.
4. **Peer mentorship credit** (offered): If a student opts into the contributor role and demonstrates reliable delivery, they earn "Peer Mentor" credit (1% bonus) towards a future course.

**For students aiming to exceed the ceiling (advanced):**
1. **Extended role**: Encouraged to take product ownership roles: backlog lead, tech lead, demo lead (one per sprint).
2. **Reflection paper**: Final reflection artifact prompts deeper introspection; guided reading on product strategy (optional: Cagan's "Empowered" chapters).
3. **Internship fast-track** (if available): Possibility of a 2-month pre-internship project (full-time, sem 8, parallel to capstone) with a SIG partner; real user traction required.
4. **Entrepreneurship path** (if available): Advanced students proposing a venture (rather than a hired contributor role) can elect to ship a product in capstone; graded on market fit + unit economics + team execution (different rubric, same credits).

## 21. Success metrics (SIG-level dashboard)

**Cohort-level outcomes (tracked post-course, SIG annual review):**
1. **Shipping discipline:** % of student teams that achieve a production-ready release by sprint 11 (target: 80%+).
2. **Hiring conversion:** % of studio graduates hired within 6 months of graduation in "product engineering" or "software engineer II" roles at target recruiters (target: 60%+).
3. **Internship quality:** Feedback from internship supervisors on studio grads' ability to own and ship features (target: 4.5+ / 5.0 NPS).
4. **Reflection & career clarity:** % of studio graduates reporting (in post-course survey) that the studio clarified their career path (PM vs. IC vs. founder) (target: 70%+).
5. **CGPA signal validity:** Verify that studio O/A+ grades correlate with hiring success (internship sponsorship, offer conversion) and advanced students do contribute more to shipping (target: Spearman ρ > 0.6).

## 22. 🔵 Merrill's Five Moments of Learning (pedagogical strategy)

**How the studio session plan operationalizes Merrill's learning phases:**

| Merrill phase | Goal | Studio realization | When |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Problem-centred** | Learner sees a real problem worth solving. | Curate a product brief from recruiter needs; present on day 1 with user pain point & market signal. Role-play: "This is the problem Flipkart faced in 2024…" | Week 0 (onboarding) + sprint 1 |
| **2. Activation** | Recall prior knowledge; frame the new problem in context. | Sprint 1 kickoff: "Recall our user interviews from PE3; how does that apply here?" Live demo of a prior studio product (shipped by last year's cohort). | Sprint 1 |
| **3. Demonstration** | Model the skill / solution approach. | Faculty live-codes a feature (rough, deliberately); shows backlog refinement; models a user interview (role-play); mentors live-review a PR. | Sprints 1–4 |

## 23. Closing reflection for faculty

**This course works if:**
- You curate a brief that students **believe** is real and worth solving.
- You enforce **sprint discipline** (1-week cadence is non-negotiable; enables the "ship often" learning).
- You **celebrate shipping**, not perfection. A rough but shipped feature teaches more than a polished prototype that never ships.
- You make peer feedback **psychological safe**: feedback is about the work, always; conflicts are your job to resolve, not the team's.
- You **reflect alongside students**: what did *your* product intuitions get wrong? Model lifelong learning.

**Red flags (course is in trouble if):**
- Teams are still in discovery / planning mode in sprint 6. ← Sprint planning is taking too long; simplify the brief or narrow the scope.
- A team ships nothing by sprint 11. ← Intervene in sprint 5; re-staff the team or re-scope the brief.
- Peer feedback is silent or uniformly positive. ← Psychological safety is missing; normalise constructive critique in a team meeting.
- Students see this as "group project homework." ← Emphasize hiring signal and recruiting alignment; invite mentors to demo day; connect to internships.
